AI is not automation. AI is not an algorithm. Those are both predictable, and AI is anything but.  

If you want predictable and repeatable business workflows, you probably don't want AI. You want a process, a pipeline, a computer program. Inputs, defined process steps, and outputs.  

Greater minds than me will continue to debate the true essence of "intelligence" as it relates to AI, but I'm comfortable with accepting that these neural networks reason and think in ways that closely mimic real human brains. Based on that, we should think of AI models more like we do a staff member.  

Real human staff members need to be trained, given instructions and shown what to do. They come to the role with prior knowledge and experiences that will flavour every choice they make and every action they take. Given the exact same set of instructions, two people might tackle a task differently, with subtly different outcomes - perhaps even wildly different. In some cases, they may even mis-interpret the instructions, go off on a tangent or just make a mistake.  

To give an example from the world of GIS where I spend most of my time. If you ask me to build a data pipeline, unless you specify otherwise, I'm more likely to do it in **Python**, because that's the bulk of my experience. I have colleages for whom the obvious choice would be to build it in **FME**. Someone with no experience at all might attempt it in **JavaScript**! The point being, you would typically make sure to specify to me or my colleague your expectations and provide context. You don't have FME? Or perhaps you do have FME and it's already used for all your other data pipelines? This is clearly important context that we would need to know in order to deliver a pipeline that works best for you. If you were to task an AI Agent with builing this data pipeline, you should provide similar information, otherwise the AI will have to decide for itself, and who's to say it doesn't go off and build it using **C++** or **Rust** because it thinks performance should be the defining factor?!  

But here's the kicker. If you asked me to build that data pipeline, and then a year later you asked me to build it again from scratch without referring to the first one, it's highly likely that my second Python script would be different to the first. This is the difference between an **intelligence** and an **algorithm**. Me re-building that Python script from scratch is one thing. However, if I were to literally copy/paste the code from one text editor to another, that is a **computer algorithm** at work. Entirely predictable. Same outcome every time. I could copy/paste that script a million times and, except perhaps for rare edge cases such as memory corruption etc, you will get the same script a million times. But ask me to re-write it? And you'll likely get a million variations. Hopefully most of them work.  

There is a fun online game that has been around a few years called [Gandalf, from Lakera](https://gandalf.lakera.ai/). In it, your goal is to make _Gandalf_ reveal the secrent password for each level. _Gandalf_ is of course, an LLM. Here in New Zealand, there was a news story came out recently about an [AI chatbot used by Health NZ](https://newsroom.co.nz/2026/03/20/health-nz-downplays-security-flaw-found-in-its-vaunted-ai-chatbot/) that was "hacked" in a similar way to that Gandalf game. Or was it hacked? Not really, according to the security lead for the product. I can't comment authoratatively on the actual product or use case of course, but I can see the theme of this blog post playing out here. 

Health New Zealand's AI Agent, dubbed **"Heidi"**, was tasked with helping scribe patient notes. The issue here is whether you view this as a dictation recording computer program, or scribing helper. If you literally want what the doctor says to be recorded, converted to text and saved, then there are computer programs that have existed for a long time with very complex and predictable algoritms that will do this. Some of those algorithms are better than others for sure, but generally speaking, what is said is reflected in the output text.  

But what if you want more than that? What if the doctor gives a long description of a problem and you want the documentation to include a short summary? Now, if you ask two human assistants to summarise something, you'll likely get two different summaries. If you give them the same notes a month later and ask them to summarise them again without referring back to their original summaries, you'll likely get another two slighly different summaries. And you'll get a similar experience with AI Agents. You can debate the quality of the summaries provided by the humans and the AI, but the fact remains - the summary text is not an entirely predictable outcome.  

But then the implications start. If you have a human assistant, and perhaps a news journalist takes them out for a drink one night and tries to trick them into revealing patient information that they might have inadvertantly learnt at work. That human has specifically been told during their work onboarding and training that:

```bash
Patient confidentiality is paramount and you should NEVER reveal patient information
```

But the journalist might still be able to trick or blackmail that person into revealing some information. And the same could be said of an AI Agent. It may have all the system prompts and guardrails but who's to say that some CIA level interrogation techniques might figure out how to make Gandalf spill the beans? _**If you want to definitively control the output, then you want an algorithm, not an AI**_. And conversely, if you use an AI, then you can never 100% predict the output.  

Does this mean AI is bad? Is AI wrong for that Health New Zealand use case? This will continue to be debated long and hard and I certainly don't claim to have a good answer. But my point is simply that we should begin to think of the AI Agents in a similar fashion to a person. Is it acceptable that an AI Agent might reveal confidential patient information? Well, is it acceptable that a human might reveal confidential patient information? Of course not, in either case. The reality is that no one should be attempting to get that information of out either humans or AI.  

The real question here of course is accountability. We can hold the human accountable for spilling the beans when they've been told not to, but it's a little harder to hold an AI Agent accountable. However, there is value to be had from using AI Agents. If AI Agents consistently do a better job at summarising doctor's notes than a human and that level of quality can be consistently scaled out across thousands of doctors then I can imagine there would be tremendous benefit for the health system in that.    

Algorithms and automated workflows are both predictable. If all you want for your business is 100% guaranteed predictable outcomes, then this is want you want. A defined process, a repeatable data pipeline, a decision chart.  

_**AI is all about moving your business into the realm of unpredictability**_. Tapping into the value that comes from insight, analysis and reasoning. You just need to weigh up the value vs the risk, likely in a similar fashion to any time you contemplate hiring a new staff member!
