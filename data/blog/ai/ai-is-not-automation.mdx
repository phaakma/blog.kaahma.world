# AI vs Automation  

AI is not just automation, and it is not merely an algorithm in the traditional sense. Unlike deterministic software systems, modern AI systems exhibit probabilistic, context‑dependent behaviour that is difficult to fully predict. This has shifted Symbolic AI and related approaches out of the spotlight, even though many modern systems still rely on symbolic techniques behind the scenes.  

If all you want is predictable and repeatable business workflows, you might not actually be wanting AI. You want a process, a pipeline, a computer program. Defined inputs, process steps, and outputs.  

There is certainly a spectrum where algorithms turn into systems turn into Artificial Intelligence. This is not a technical blog post, more a perspective on expectations. My suggestion is to think of the modern generative AI models much more like you would any staff member.  

# Algorithms vs Intelligence  

Real human staff members need to be trained, given instructions, provided tools and shown what to do. They come to the role with prior knowledge and experiences that will flavour every choice they make and every action they take. Given the exact same set of instructions, two people might tackle a task differently with different outcomes. In some cases, they may even mis-interpret the instructions, go off on a tangent or just make a mistake. Just like staff, AI agents can be constrained by policies, reviews, and tools — but never reduced to perfectly deterministic behaviour.   

To give an example from the world of GIS where I spend most of my time. If you ask me to build a data pipeline, unless you specify otherwise, I'm more likely to do it in **Python**, because that's the bulk of my experience. I have colleagues for whom the obvious choice would be to build it in **FME**. Someone with no experience at all might attempt it in **JavaScript**! The point being, you would typically make sure to specify to me or my colleague your expectations and provide context. You don't have FME? Or perhaps you do have FME and it's already used for all your other data pipelines? This is clearly important context that we would need to know in order to deliver a pipeline that works best for you. If you were to task an AI Agent with building this data pipeline, you should provide similar information, otherwise the AI will have to decide for itself, and who's to say it doesn't go off and build it using **C++** or **Rust** because the model infers that performance might be the dominant requirement.    

But here's the kicker. If you asked me to build that data pipeline, and then a year later you asked me to build it again from scratch without referring to the first one, it's highly likely that my second Python script would be different to the first. This illustrates one of the key differences between an **intelligence** and a traditional **algorithm**. Me re-building that Python script from scratch is one thing. However, if I were to literally copy/paste the code from one text editor to another, that is a **computer algorithm** at work. Entirely predictable. Same outcome every time. I could copy/paste that script a million times and get the same script a million times. But ask me to re-write it? You'll likely get a million variations. The difference is not randomness, but the ability to reason from context, experience, and intent rather than simply executing instructions.    

# AI as a Staff Member  

There is a fun online game that has been around a few years called [Gandalf, from Lakera](https://gandalf.lakera.ai/). In it, your goal is to make _Gandalf_ reveal the secret password for each level. _Gandalf_ is of course, an LLM. Here in New Zealand, a news story came out recently about an [AI chatbot used by Health NZ](https://newsroom.co.nz/2026/03/20/health-nz-downplays-security-flaw-found-in-its-vaunted-ai-chatbot/) that was prompt-manipulated in a similar fashion to the Gandalf game.  

Health New Zealand's AI Agent, dubbed **"Heidi"**, was tasked with helping scribe patient notes. The question here is whether you view this task as a dictation recording or a scribing assistant. If you literally want what the doctor says to be recorded, converted to text and saved, then there are computer programs that have existed for a long time with very complex and predictable algorithms that will do this. Some of those algorithms are better than others for sure, but generally speaking, what is said is reflected in the output text.  

But what if you want more than that? What if the doctor gives a long description of a problem and you want the documentation to include a short summary? Now, if you ask two human assistants to summarise something, you'll likely get two different summaries. If you give them the same notes a month later and ask them to summarise them again without referring back to their original summaries, you'll likely get another two slightly different summaries. And you'll get a similar experience with AI Agents. You can debate the quality of the summaries provided by the humans and the AI, but the fact remains - the summary text is not an entirely predictable outcome.  

# Predictability and Risk  

But then the implications start. Say you have a human assistant and a news journalist takes them out for a drink one night and tries to trick them into revealing patient information that they might have inadvertantly learnt at work. That human has specifically been told during their work onboarding and training that:

`Patient confidentiality is paramount and you should NEVER reveal patient information`

But the journalist might still be able to trick or blackmail that person into revealing some information. And the same could be said of an AI Agent. It may have all the system prompts and guardrails but who's to say that sufficiently sophisticated social‑engineering techniques might figure out how to make Gandalf spill the beans? _**If you want absolute, formal guarantees over every possible output, then you want a deterministic algorithm, not a generative model**_. And conversely, in this sort of example, if you use an LLM, then you can never 100% predict the output.  

Does this mean AI is bad? Is AI wrong for that Health New Zealand use case? This will continue to be debated long and hard and I certainly don't claim to have a good answer. But my point is simply that we should begin to think of the AI Agents in a similar fashion to a person. Is it acceptable that an AI Agent might reveal confidential patient information? Well, is it acceptable that a human might reveal confidential patient information? Of course not, in either case. The reality is that no one should be attempting to get that information of out either humans or AI.  

# Accountability  

The real question here of course is accountability. We can hold the human accountable for spilling the beans when they've been told not to, but it's a little harder to hold an AI Agent accountable. Accountability therefore shifts from the agent to system design, governance, and risk management — which is ultimately a human responsibility. However, there is value to be had from using AI Agents. If AI Agents consistently do a better job at summarising doctors’ notes than humans, and that level of quality can be scaled across thousands of clinicians, there could be tremendous benefit for the health system. The challenge for organisations is how to design systems such that neither humans nor AI agents are ever placed in a position where disclosure is possible in the first place.  

# Where AI Actually Adds Value  

_**AI can move parts of your business into a space where outcomes are not fully specifiable in advance — and where new insights can emerge**_. And that's where the real value lives. Using insight, analysis and reasoning from AI to find out what you didn't know before. You just need to weigh up the value vs the risk, likely in a similar fashion to any time you contemplate hiring a new staff member!  

A final thought that brings both together. You might hire someone to design a new process or improve an existing system. You can use generative AI to do the same, and perhaps that is what gets you to the place you want to be? Your end goal is a reliable, predictable business process, and the way you got there was to use AI.
