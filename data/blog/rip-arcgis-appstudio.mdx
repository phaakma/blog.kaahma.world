It's been almost ten years exactly since I first heard of [ArcGIS AppStudio](https://developers.arcgis.com/appstudio/). 
I was working for a small 
company called [GPSit](https://www.gpsit.co.nz/) who at the time was searching for a replacement platform for a mobile geospatial 
app built using the ArcGIS for Windows Mobile SDK. This app was used by [Fonterra](https://www.fonterra.com/) field staff to [map 
out waterways on farms](https://www.fonterra.com/nz/en/our-stories/media/fonterra-farmers-achieve-20400km-fence-waterways.html) to record their fencing status. I attended the Esri Developer Summit in 
Palm Springs in March 2015, and quite by chance we noticed a pre-conference workshop on **Creating 
Cross-Platform Native Apps with ArcGIS AppStudio**. I attended the workshop and immediately the 
concept clicked as a perfect fit for us at the time.

ArcGIS AppStudio provided the SDK and frameworks along with taking care of the build process, 
allowing us to focus primarily on application logic and UI. Upon returning from that Esri Developer 
Summit, with zero prior experience with Qt or QML or ArcGIS AppStudio, within 3 months we had a 
working mobile application for Fonterra that worked on both iPads and Windows laptops and a very 
happy customer. I don't know how we would have achieved that at the time without ArcGIS AppStudio.

Over the following years, the mobile app ([which became known as the 'Tiaki App' and part of the 
wider Tiaki campaign](https://www.fonterra.com/nz/en/campaign/tiaki.html)) was enhanced with more spatial drawing tools and custom workflows encompassing 
riparian plantings, nitrogen reporting and Farm Environment Plan generation. I particularly enjoyed 
writing a tool that made it easy to digitize farm irrigation pivots by drawing a circle, 
defining the radius dynamically or by specific measurements, and optionally to create a wedge 
shape since many pivots are not full circles. The tool automatically clipped itself back to the 
farm paddock to exclude waterways and other areas.

No one would have known, but in the actual code that tool was called **PacMan**, because the drawn 
shapes often resembled our favourite retro game character! Over the years, many PowerPoint 
presentations by GPSit at dozens of conferences included screenshots of polygons drawn with this tool.

More AppStudio projects followed, with a clear highlight for me being the opportunity to build
the [official mobile application](https://www.waikato.com/whats-on/news/2018/05/14/new-free-app-means-it-is-easier-and-quicker-to-navigate-fieldays) 
for the [New Zealand National Fieldays](https://www.fieldays.co.nz/) in 2018 and 2019. 
As a massive event within NZ with large numbers of visitors in a very short period, I recall 
we hit #2 spot in both the Apple and Google NZ app stores in 2018, only missing out on the top 
spot due to a slightly larger event occurring at the same time - the FIFA World Cup! 
Being a free publicly available mobile app, the Fieldays app presented new challenges 
around user expectations and support. Feedback varied from *'this is awesome'* to 
*'this is absolutely the worst app ever'*, but with tens of thousands of downloads and 
only a handful of negative responses, the overall reception seemed good. My main 
learning from this project was to allow plenty of hours for post-go-live hyper-care as 
free public apps will always throw up unexpected issues.

Since joining [Eagle Technology](https://www.eagle.co.nz/) four years ago, I helped [Herenga ā Nuku Aotearoa](https://www.herengaanuku.govt.nz/) create an 
AppStudio app called [Pocket Maps](https://www.herengaanuku.govt.nz/maps/pocket-maps) which has been well received and even won it's creators 
a [Special Achievement in GIS Award which they travelled to the 2022 Esri User 
Conference San Diego to receive](https://www.herengaanuku.govt.nz/our-work/news/news/maps-take-award-winning-kiwi-across-the-world). 

These have been my highlights in my journey with ArcGIS AppStudio. The Esri AppStudio team
 have been fantastic to deal with over the past decade, and I'm grateful to them for their 
 help and support. AppStudio had a lot to do with shaping my career at the time. It brought 
 my field geospatial data collection work together with my interests in IT and computing, 
 revived my enthusiasm for coding and app making, and challenged me to do a deep dive into 
 the world of GIS and spatial tools which led me towards my current role as a GIS Advisor.

ArcGIS AppStudio is now retired. If you are looking for a way to replace your ArcGIS AppStudio 
apps with a cross-platform option, check out any of the other **ArcGIS Maps SDKs**, including .NET Maui or the recently out 
of beta Flutter SDK. Or visit this [Esri blog on various options available to you](https://www.esri.com/arcgis-blog/products/appstudio/announcements/arcgis-appstudio-retirement-how-to-ensure-a-smooth-transition/).
